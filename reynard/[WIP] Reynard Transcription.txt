The History of Reynard the Foxe
<pb/> Hyer begynneth the hystorye of Reynard the Foxe<g=flower><g=flower><f=flower> In thys hystorye ben wreton the parables, goode lerynge, and dyuerse poyntes to be merkyed, by whiche poyntes men maye lerne to come to the subtylle knoweleche of suche thynges as dayly ben vsed and had in the counseyllys of lordes and prelates, gostely and worldly, and also emonge marchantes &amp; other comone peple. And this booke is maade for nede and prouffyte of alle god folke, as fer as they in redynge or heeryng of it shall mowe vnderstand and fele the forsaid subtil deceytes that dayly ben vsed in the worlde, not to thentente that men shold vse them, but that euery man sholde eschewe and kepe hym from the subtyl false shrewys, that they be not deceyuyd<g=spiky>Thenne who that will haue the very vnderstandyng of thys matter, he muste ofte and many tymes rede in thys booke, and ernestly and diligently marke well that he redeth, for it is sette subtylly, lyke as ye shal see in redyng of it; and not ones to rede it; for a man shall not wyth ones ouer redyng fynde the right vnderstandyng, ne comprise it wel, but oftymes to rede it shal cause it wel to be vnderstande: and for them that vnderstandeth it, it shall be ryght joyous, playsant, and proyffitable.<pb> Howe the Lyon, kynge of alle bestis, sent out hys mandementis that alle beestis sholde come to his feest and court, capitulo primo<g=flower><g=flower> It was about the time of Penthecoste or Whytsontyde, that the wodes comynly be lusty and gladsom, and the trees clad with leuys and blossoms, and the grounde with herbes &amp; flowris swete smellyng, &amp; also the fowles &amp; byrdes syngen melodyously in theyr armonye, that the Lyon, the noble kynge of all beestis, wolde in the holy dayes of thys feest holde an open court at Stade, whyche he dide to knowe ouer alle in hys lande. And comanded by strayte conmyssyons &amp; maundements that euery beest shold come thyder, in such wyse that alle the beestis grete &amp; smale cam to the courte, sauf Reynard the Fox, for he knewe himself fawty &amp; gylty in many thinges ayenst many beestis that thyder sholde comen, that he durste not auenture to goo thyder<g=spiky> Whan the kynge of alle beestis has assemblid all his court, ther was none of them alle but that he had complayned sore on Reynart the Foxe. The firste complaynt made Isegrym the Wulf on Reynart, capirulo ij<g=flower><g=flower> Isegrym the Wulf wyth his lynage and frendes cam and stode to fore the kynge, &amp; sayde<g=spiky> Hye and myghty prynce, my lord the hynge, I beseche yow that thurgh your grete mygtht, ryght, &amp; mercy, that ye wyl haue pyte on the grete trespas &amp; the vnreasonable mys<pb/>dedes that Reynart the Foxe hath don to me &amp; to my wyf.

And yet hath he trespaced to me in many other thinges, he is not lyuyng that coude telle alle that I now leue vntolde; but the shame and vyllonye that he hath don to my wyf, that shal I neuer hyde ne suffre it vn-auengyd, but that he shal make to me large amendes.

For sothe, my lord the kynge, yf ye suffre this vnpunysshyd, and lete hym go quyte that hath thus broken your peas, and wyl do no right after the sentence &amp; juge-<pb/>ment of your men, your chyldren many yeris herafter shal be myspreysed and blamed therfore <g=spiky> Sykerly Panther, sayd Isegrym, ye saye trouthe! Now complayneth Courtoys that he with payne had goten a puddyng in the wynter at suche tyme as the coste is euyl to fynde. Therof hym had he better to have holde his pees, for he had stolen it; male quesisti et male perdisti, hit is ryght that it be evil loste that is evil wonne. Who shal blame Reynart yf he have taken fro a theef stolen good? Hit is reson, who that vnderstandeth tha lawe and can discerne the right, &amp; that <pb/>he be of hye burthe as myn eme Reynart is, whiche knoweth wel how he shal resseyue stolen good; ye, al had he Courtys hanged whan he fonde hym wyth the menowr, he hadde not moche mysdon ne trespaced, sauf ayenst the crowne, that he had don justyse wythoute leue, wherfore for the honour of the kynge he dyde it not, all hath he but lytel thanke. What skathed it hym, that he is thus complayned on? Myn eme is a gentil &amp; a treweman, he may suffre no falshede; he doth nothynge but by hys prestes counseyl, &amp; I saye yowsyth that my lorde the kynge hath do proclamed his pees he neuer thoughte to hurte onyman, for he eteth no more than a yere that he hath eten no flesshe, as I yesterday herd saye of them that cam fro hym; he hath lefte and geuen ouer his castlel Malerperduys &amp;hath bylded a cluse, theryn dwelleth he &amp;hunteth nomore, ne desyreth no wynnynge, but he lyueth by almesse and taketh nothyng but ushce as men gyue hym for charyte, and doth grete penance for his synnes, and he is woxen moche pale and lene of prayeng and wakyng for he wolde be fayn wyth God. <g=spiky> Thus as Grymbert his eme stode and preched thise wordes, so sawe they comen doun the hylle to hem Chauntecler the Cock, and brought on a biere a deed henne of whom Reynart had byten the heed of, and that muste be shewed to the kynge for to haue knowleche therof.

Chauntecler came forth &amp; smote pyteously his handes and his fetheris, and on eche side of the byer wenten tweyne sorouful hennes; that one was called Cantart, and that other goode henne Crayant, they were two the fairest hennes that were bitwene Holland and Arderne. Thise hennes bare eche of them brennyng tapre whiche was longe and strayte, thise two hennes were Coppens susters and they cryed so pisuster Coppen. Two yonge hennes bare the byere, whiche kakled so heuyly and wepte  so lowde for the deth of Coppen their moder that it was ferre herde. Thus cam they to gydre to fore the kynge, &amp; Chantecleer tho seyde <g=spiky> Merciful Lord, my lord the kyng, plese it yow to here our complaynte and abhorren the grete scathe that reynart hath don to me &amp; my children that hiere stonden. It was so that in the begynnyng of Appryl whan the weder is fayr, as that I was hardy and prowde, bycause of the greete lynage that I am comen of, and also hadde, for I had viij fayr sones and seuen fayr  doughters whiche mywyf had hatched, and they were alle stronge and fatte, &amp; wente in a yerde whiche was walled round a/boute, in whiche was a shadde where in were six grete dogges whiche was had to/tore and pluckes many a beestis skyn in suche wyse as my chyldren were not aferd. On whom Reynart the theef had grete enuye by cause <pb/>they were so sure that he cowde none gete of them, how wel oftymes hath this fel theef goon rounde aboute this wal, and hath leyde for vs in suche wyse that the dogges haue be sette on hym &amp; haue hunted hym away. And ones they leep on hym vpon the banke, and that cost hym somwhat for his thefte. I saw that his skyn smoked; nevertheless he wente his waye, God amende it.

<pb/>Thus were we quyte of Reynart a longe whyle. Atte laste cam he in lynkes of an heremyte, &amp; brought to me a lettre for to rede, sealed wythe the kynges seal, in whiche stode wreton that the kynge had made pees oueral in his royame, &amp; that alle maner bestis and fowlles shold doo none harme ner scathe to ony other. Yet sayd he to me more that he was a cloysterer or closyd recluse be-comen, and that he wolde receyue grete penance for his synnes. He shewd me his slauyne &amp; pylche, and an heren sherte ther vnder, &amp; thenne sayd he <g=spiky>Syr Chaunteclere, after thys tyme be no more aferd of me, ne take no hede, for I now will ete no more flessh; I am forthon so olde that I wolde fayn remembre my sowle. I wil now go forth, for I haue yete to saye my sexte, none, and myn euensonge; to God I by-take yow <g=round>Tho wente Reynart thens, sayeng his Credo, and leyde hym vnder an hawthorn. Thenne was I glad and mery, and also toke none hede, and wente to my chyldren and clucked hem to gyder, and wente wythout the wal for to walke, wherof is moche harme comen to vs; for Reynart laye vnder a busshe and cam krepyng bitwene vs and the yate, so that he caght one of my chyldren and leyd hym in his male, <pb/>wherof whe haue had grete harme, for syth he hath tasted of hym ther myght neuer hunter ne hounde saue ne kepe hym from vs. He hath wayted by nyghte &amp; daye in such wyse that he hath stolen so many of my chyldren that of xv I haue but foure, in suche wyse hath this theef forslogen them. And yet yes-terday was Coppen my doughter, that hier lyeth vp-on the byer, with the houndes rescowed. this complayne I to yow, gracious kynge; haue pyte on myn grete &amp; vnresonable damage and losse of my fayre chyldren.
